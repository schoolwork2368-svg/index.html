Universal Equations Guide

In-depth explanations for Mathematics, Physics, and Advanced Concepts.

This rule dictates the sequence of mathematical operations to ensure consistent results. Without it, a single equation could have many different answers.

BEDMAS: Brackets, Exponents, Division/Multiplication, Addition/Subtraction

PEMDAS: Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication/Division, Addition/Subtraction

In-Depth Explanation:

  • **Brackets/Parentheses (B/P):** Always resolve expressions inside grouping symbols first.
  • **Exponents (E):** Calculate powers and roots next.
  • **Division and Multiplication (DM):** These have equal priority. You must solve them from **left to right** in the equation.
  • **Addition and Subtraction (AS):** These also have equal priority. Solve them from **left to right**.

**Example Equation:**

10 - 2 × 3 + 6 / 2

1. **DM (Left to Right):** 2 × 3 = 6 and 6 / 2 = 3. The equation becomes: 10 - 6 + 3

2. **AS (Left to Right):** 10 - 6 = 4. The equation becomes: 4 + 3 = 7

Solving an equation means finding the value of the unknown variable (usually **x**) that makes the two sides of the equation equal. This is achieved through **inverse operations**.

The Inverse Operation Principle:

For any equation, whatever operation you perform on one side of the equals sign, you must perform the exact same operation on the other side to maintain the balance.

  • Addition's inverse is Subtraction, and vice versa.
  • Multiplication's inverse is Division, and vice versa.
  • Squaring's inverse is the Square Root (√), and vice versa.

**Example Equation:**

3x + 7 = 19

1. **Isolate the term with x:** Subtract 7 from both sides: 3x = 19 - 7 -> 3x = 12

2. **Isolate x:** Divide both sides by 3: x = 12 / 3 -> x = 4

The goal is always to 'undo' the operations surrounding **x** in reverse-BEDMAS order.

Fractions represent a part of a whole, where the numerator is the count of parts and the denominator is the total number of parts in the whole. (e.g., 1/2 means 1 part out of a total of 2 parts).

Addition/Subtraction:

You MUST have a **Common Denominator (LCD)** before adding or subtracting.

**Equation:**

1/3 + 1/4

The LCD of 3 and 4 is 12. Adjust the fractions: 4/12 + 3/12 = 7/12

Multiplication:

Multiply the numerators together and the denominators together (no common denominator needed!)

2/3 × 5/7 = (2×5) / (3×7) = 10/21

Division:

The rule is "Keep, Change, Flip." Keep the first fraction, change division to multiplication, and flip (take the reciprocal of) the second fraction.

3/5 ÷ 2/7 = 3/5 × 7/2 = 21/10

A polynomial is an expression consisting of variables and coefficients, involving only the operations of addition, subtraction, multiplication, and non-negative integer exponents. **FOIL** is a mnemonic device for multiplying two **binomials** (two-term polynomials).

FOIL: First, Outer, Inner, Last

**Equation (General Form):**

(a + b) × (c + d)
  • **F (First):** a × c = ac
  • **O (Outer):** a × d = ad
  • **I (Inner):** b × c = bc
  • **L (Last):** b × d = bd

**Resulting Equation:** ac + ad + bc + bd

**Example:** (x + 2) × (x - 5) = x^2 - 5x + 2x - 10 = x^2 - 3x - 10

Linear equations are typically graphed using the **Slope-Intercept Form**, which immediately reveals the line's starting point and direction.

y = mx + b

  • **y and x:** The coordinates of any point on the line.
  • **m (Slope):** The "rise over run" (change in y / change in x), which determines the steepness and direction of the line.
  • **b (y-Intercept):** The point where the line crosses the y-axis (the y value when x=0).

How the Equation Works:

The equation y=mx+b states that the final y-value of any point is the fixed starting point (**b**) plus the amount of change (**mx**) that occurs as you move horizontally (**x**) based on the rate of change (**m**).

**Example:** Graphing y = 2x - 1

1. **Start:** The y-intercept (**b**) is -1. Plot the point (0, -1).

2. **Move:** The slope (**m**) is 2, or 2/1. From (0, -1), move up 2 units (rise) and right 1 unit (run) to find the next point, (1, 1). Connect the points.

Kinematics is the study of motion. A fundamental equation relates final velocity, initial velocity, acceleration, and time.

v_f = v_i + a × t

  • **v_f**: Final velocity (m/s)
  • **v_i**: Initial velocity (m/s)
  • **a**: Constant acceleration (m/s²)
  • **t**: Time interval (s)

How the Equation Works:

The core definition of acceleration (**a**) is how much velocity changes (Δv) over time (**t**). Since Δv is v_f - v_i, we have: a = (v_f - v_i) / t

When rearranged, the equation shows that your final velocity (**v_f**) is equal to your starting velocity (**v_i**) plus the total change in velocity (**a × t**).

The speed required for a satellite to maintain a circular orbit around a large body (like Earth) is determined by the gravitational force.

v = √( (G × M) / r )

  • **v**: Orbital velocity (m/s)
  • **G**: Gravitational constant (a fixed number: $6.674 \times 10^{-11}$)
  • **M**: Mass of the central body (kg) (e.g., Earth)
  • **r**: Radius of the orbit (m) (distance from the center of mass)

How the Equation Works:

This equation comes from balancing the **centripetal force** (the force needed to keep an object moving in a circle) and the **gravitational force** pulling the objects together. After canceling the satellite's mass, we are left with this formula. It shows that faster orbits happen closer to the central mass (**r** is small) or around heavier objects (**M** is large).

Albert Einstein's famous equation from the theory of Special Relativity. It defines the relationship between mass and energy.

E = m × c^2

  • **E**: Energy (Joules, J)
  • **m**: Mass (kilograms, kg)
  • **c**: Speed of light in a vacuum (a very large fixed number: $300,000,000$ m/s)

In-Depth Explanation:

The equation states that **mass (m) is a highly concentrated form of energy (E)**, and they can be converted into one another. Because the speed of light (**c**) is so large, and it is squared ($c^2$), it means a tiny amount of mass can be converted into an enormous amount of energy, which is the principle behind nuclear power.

Calculus measures **rates of change**. The derivative ($f'(x)$) finds the instantaneous slope of a function at a single point, known as the **slope of the tangent line**.

f'(x) = lim (h -> 0) [ (f(x+h) - f(x)) / h ]

  • **$\lim_{h\to 0}$**: The limit as $h$ approaches zero.
  • **$f(x+h) - f(x)$**: The change in the $y$ value (the "rise").
  • **$h$**: The change in the $x$ value (the "run").

How the Equation Works:

This is the **limit definition** of the derivative. It calculates the average slope between two points, $x$ and $x+h$, and then uses the limit to squeeze those two points together, finding the slope at exactly the point $x$.

This is the most important identity in trigonometry, derived from the Pythagorean theorem applied to the **unit circle** ($x^2 + y^2 = r^2$).

sin²($\theta$) + cos²($\theta$) = 1

  • **$\sin\theta$**: The $y$-coordinate on the unit circle.
  • **$\cos\theta$**: The $x$-coordinate on the unit circle.

How the Equation Works:

Since the hypotenuse of the triangle formed in the unit circle is always 1 (the radius), and since $\sin\theta = y/1$ and $\cos\theta = x/1$, squaring and adding the sine and cosine must always equal $1^2$, or **1**.

The quadratic formula solves for the roots (or **x-intercepts**) of *any* quadratic equation in standard form: $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$.

x = [-b $\pm$ sqrt(b² - 4ac)] / 2a

Key Components:

  • **$\pm$ (Plus/Minus):** Indicates that there are usually two solutions (roots).
  • **$b^2 - 4ac$ (The Discriminant):** This part tells you how many solutions exist. If it's positive, two real solutions. If it's zero, one solution. If it's negative, no real solutions.

This formula describes a fundamental property of all simple, convex polyhedra (like cubes, pyramids, or soccer balls).

V - E + F = 2

  • **V**: Number of **Vertices** (corners).
  • **E**: Number of **Edges** (lines connecting corners).
  • **F**: Number of **Faces** (flat surfaces).

Example (Cube):

A cube has 8 vertices, 12 edges, and 6 faces. $8 - 12 + 6 = 2$. The relationship always holds true!

The Z-score (or standard score) tells you exactly how many **standard deviations** a specific value ($x$) is away from the average (mean, $\mu$).

z = (x - $\mu$) / $\sigma$

  • **$x$**: The raw data value.
  • **$\mu$ (Mu)**: The mean (average) of the data set.
  • **$\sigma$ (Sigma)**: The standard deviation (how spread out the data is).

Purpose:

The Z-score allows you to compare data points from completely different sets. For example, you can use it to compare a test score from a math class to a score from a history class.

Combinations ($C(n, k)$) count the number of ways to choose $k$ items from a larger set of $n$ items, **when the order doesn't matter**.

C(n, k) = n! / [k! (n-k)!]

  • **$n$**: Total number of items in the set.
  • **$k$**: Number of items to choose.
  • **$!$ (Factorial):** $n! = n \times (n-1) \times \dots \times 1$.

Example:

How many ways can you choose a 3-person team from 10 students? This is $C(10, 3)$, because picking Jane, Joe, then Sue is the same as picking Sue, Jane, then Joe.

This is a famous **Partial Differential Equation (PDE)** that describes how waves—like sound, light, or a vibrating string—move through space and time.

u_tt = c² u_xx

  • **$u$**: The displacement (height) of the wave.
  • **$u_{tt}$**: How the wave's displacement changes over time (acceleration).
  • **$u_{xx}$**: How the wave curves in space.
  • **$c$**: The speed of the wave.

Physical Meaning:

The equation shows that the acceleration of a wave part (its curvature in time) is directly proportional to its curvature in space. This relationship is what allows waves to travel and keep their shape.

Modular arithmetic is the mathematics of "wrapping around," similar to how a clock works. It's used in computer science for things like cryptography.

a $\equiv$ b (mod m)

  • **$a$ and $b$**: The two numbers being compared.
  • **$m$**: The modulus (the number you divide by).
  • **$\equiv$**: The symbol for **congruence** (not just equality).

Example:

The equation $15 \equiv 3 \pmod{12}$ is true because when you divide 15 by 12, the remainder is 3. Similarly, 3 divided by 12 also has a remainder of 3. On a 12-hour clock, 15:00 is the same as 3:00.
